Increasing Checkout Conversion by Incentivizing Point Payments for Logged-In Users.

My Role: UX Designer
Timeline: 3 month
Tools:
What I did: Research How might we... Ideation Design

When users keep coming back but ignore their rewards, something is broken.

That’s the insight that launched my journey to redesign the rewards points experience for a large e-commerce platform. While loyal customers were consistently earning points, very few actually redeemed them. Even more alarming? Many weren’t even logging in—missing out on key benefits.

Impact
  • Reduced unnecessary driver-logistics calls by 35%
  • Service update times dropped by 97%
  • Enhanced User Experience
  • Operational costs were reduced

The Problem

Recurring users are not redeeming their accumulated points for subsequent bookings and are frequently not logging into their accounts.

Goal

Increase the percentage of users logged into our site and to boost the number of transactions paid with points.

Digging Into User Behavior

I started with usability tests and behavioral analysis, aiming to understand where and why users were disconnecting from the rewards system.

Key issues identified

  • Lack of personalization: The interface does not adapt when users are logged in, missing the opportunity to create a tailored experience.
  • Hidden information: Users must navigate to their profile to view the cash value of their points, creating unnecessary friction.
  • Unclear benefits: Exclusive rates are not perceived as member benefits, and non-logged-in users are not informed about the exclusive rates available to logged-in users. Additionally, the advantages of having an account are not clearly highlighted.
  • Equal information access: Logged-in users receive the same information as non-logged-in users, with a focus on card payments rather than promoting the use of points in either case.
  • Lack of transparency on points: Users are not informed of their available points at the beginning of the checkout process or while logged in, which reduces awareness of their options.
  • Flow Interruptions: Users are pulled out of the purchasing flow when prompted to visit their profile or create an account, disrupting the buying experience.
  • Lack of Convenience: The overall process is not simplified for logged-in users, leading to potential frustration.
  • Absence of Visual Identity: There is no clear branding for the rewards program within the interface, which could help reinforce its value to users.

Hypothesis & Testing: Designing for Clarity and Motivation

With these insights in hand, I crafted and tested several hypotheses using Optimizely and A/B testing:

Header Redesign

Hypothesis 1: Informing users about the benefits of logging in through a popup would facilitate the login process and increase the number of registered users.


However, after conducting an A/B test, we found no significant results. Determined to refine our approach, we moved on to a second hypothesis.

Hypothesis 2: If we Change the "call to action" in our button from "Log In" to "Create Account | Log In" would make it easier for new users to identify where to create an account. This version proved to be successful.


Encouraged by this outcome, we decided to take it a step further and design a version of the header specifically for logged-in users. This new header displayed the available points in their accounts, providing immediate value and encouraging engagement.

We conducted a final test in which, instead of displaying the points in the user's account, we showed the equivalent amount in currency. This change resulted in the graphs showing a significantly higher conversion rate. Previously, users were unclear about the monetary value of their points. By presenting this information, we motivated them to use their points for their next reservation and enhanced their experience by being more transparent with them.

This redesign not only improved the user experience but also reinforced the benefits of the loyalty program.

Elevating the Checkout Experience

Next, I optimized the checkout flow to make point usage frictionless and rewarding: Default to Rewards: Logged-in users had their points automatically applied at checkout, with visible discounts in their order summary. Smarter UI Module: I redesigned the points interface to show how many points they had and what discount they’d get—creating transparency and reducing cognitive load.

Hypothesis 1:If we set the payment option to points as the default for all logged-in users at checkout and display the associated discount in the purchase summary, then the number of reservations paid with points will increase as users feel more motivated to use their points.

We test this idea with an A/B testing , the new version results a winner

Hypothesis 2:If we enhance the points payment module by clearly indicating the value of the points in the user's account in currency, then users will be more likely to use their points as a payment method.

Hypothesis 3:If we display the discount for paying with points by default in the purchase summary, then users will be more motivated to use their points during checkout.

The Impact

The initiatives we implemented had a significant positive impact on user engagement and overall business performance.

OKR1 Objective Increase transactions with points to 25%

We successfully achieved this goal, rising from 12.09% of transactions paid with points in September to an average of 25% in December same year

OKR1 Objective Reaches 8% of users navigating with an active account

While this goal was ambitious and not fully met, an improvement was observed, reaching 5.87% of logged-in users.

Next steps

We continued to monitor user behavior and implemented additional actions in the next release to enhance the experience for our logged-in users and increase the number of users who created accounts in our rewards program.

  • Exclusive Offers: We introduced exclusive hotel offers specifically for logged-in users to encourage account creation and engagement.
  • Redesign of the Benefits Module: We revamped the benefits module on the homepage to clearly communicate the value of our rewards program.
  • Ongoing Enhancements: We kept enhancing the user experience during the login process, ensuring it remained seamless and inviting.

These steps were part of our commitment to creating a more engaging and rewarding experience for our users.

Take Away

  • Keep It Simple for the User: Using unclear terms can lead to a poor user experience. We learned this when we shifted our communication from points to currency. Users didn’t want to think about what the points represented; when we became clearer, our numbers increased.
  • Small Changes Make a Big Impact: It’s not necessary to overhaul an entire design to create significant effects. The value lies in the small but important details that can simplify the user experience.
  • Deliver Value Constantly: Implementing small but meaningful changes benefits the team, as feeling motivated by these quick wins sustains their energy and drive to continue iterating.
